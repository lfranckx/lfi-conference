Opening Keynote: Debugging Is A Team Sport

9:35 AM-10:00 AM

Marco Polo Ballroom

Charity Majors

Founder & CTO

Honeycomb.io

Have you ever noticed that the best debugger on a team is usually the person who’s been there the longest? This is not some kind of immutable law of engineering, it’s the direct consequence of specific choices people commonly make...and you can make different ones. What we call “debugging” is often just pattern matching on past experiences, which means you get good at debugging by experiencing similar incidents over and over and over. But what if you stop getting a ton of repeat outages? What if, whenever you get paged, it’s almost always something new? Time to systems; how to methodically interrogate your systems, putting one foot after the other by asking question after question, which is how you follow the results to the answer. Every time. Teams that are consistently good at debugging weren’t born that way; they got good by 1) using good tools, and 2) learning to leverage and piggyback off each other’s expertise.

Warning: Principal Engineer under the influence of Learning from Incidents

10:10 AM-10:35 AM

Marco Polo Ballroom

Tim Nicholas

Principal Engineer

Xero

After burning out as an Architect, learning about Learning from Incidents revitalised my enthusiasm for working in complex systems and prevented me from choosing life as a peasant farmer. It has deeply changed how I approach my work as an SRE Principal Engineer. Incidents are a key source of insight into the strengths and constraints in socio-technical systems but those direct insights are just the beginning. The tools, mindset and concepts that form the foundation of modern LFI practices have much wider applicability.

In this talk I’ll share how I came to incident learning and how, over the last 4 years, the theory and practice of LFI and Resilience Engineering have fundamentally changed where and how I seek to influence systemic change and improve site operations. Beyond the incidents, I’ll share examples relating to policy refinement, tool design, team structure, measures for reporting, and making space by being a visible advocate for people working at the sharp end.

My journey has had many overlapping stages. My initial focus was on enhancing the expertise and judgement of engineers. That developed to include understanding the trade-space engineers work within. Later still I came to appreciate the value of identifying patterns of decisions and their consequences sometimes spanning years. I now try to use those patterns to inform higher level decisions that change the options available to engineers.

Advanced Incidents and Dragons: Disaster Roleplaying Is For Everyone

10:10 AM-10:35 AM

Red Rover

Derek Remund

Reliability Engineering Manager

Google

Disaster roleplaying: everyone talks about it, but few really embrace it. It can be hard to get out of our comfort zones and engage in what might seem like a game. It's worth examining those roadblocks and learning new techniques to unlock the benefits of "Dungeons and Dragons for engineers".

Disaster roleplaying comes in many forms, and different approaches will be appropriate for teams and individuals at different times. We'll start by examining some of those approaches and how we can fit them in with our existing team activities.

No matter your approach, however, a poorly-developed or non-engaging scenario can be a recipe for boredom, or even the gradual dissolution of our rugged band of incident adventurers. We'll look at some good, and not-so-good, scenario examples, both to learn to evaluate them and to build them efficiently.

Even the most well-developed scenario can't solve, by itself, for every individuals' needs when engaging in a disaster roleplaying exercise. We want to ensure our exercises are inclusive and allow participation for as many learning types as possible. We'll examine some common inclusivity pitfalls and some creative ways to engage our entire teams.

Finally, we'll bring it all together with an example exercise and identify some critical things a good "dungeon master" must do to ensure a successful experience for all. We'll leave with a few immediate things we can do to build a good incidents culture, even before we're ready to fully embrace disaster roleplaying. Wizard hats optional, but encouraged!

The role of monitoring in proactive safety and performance management

10:45 AM-11:10 AM

Hopscotch

Christine Jefferies

PhD Candidate

The Ohio State University

This talk will discuss the differences among reactive, responsive, and proactive approaches to safety and performance monitoring. In many safety critical domains, monitoring of the process being managed is a core function to ensuring safe operations. While monitoring in the software engineering domain typically looks at technical performance of the system, in this context we include the socio-technical system, that is, monitoring the work being done and monitoring the system's ability to deliver work.

The monitoring capability of a proactive management system must be informed by at least three types of monitoring (Balkin, 2023). Firstly, capability must exist to monitor for changes in the system, which goes beyond monitoring technical performance to include, through reflective system analysis, monitoring for the violation of requirements and assumptions made during system design. Secondly, the socio-technical system must monitor the ability of the system to detect, interpret, and communicate changes to decision-making agents, which includes continuous confirmatory testing that the sampling process would in fact collect and distribute desired and useful information regarding changes to the appropriate parts of the system. Thirdly, monitoring system adaptive capacity, which Woods & Rayo (2022, p. 16) define as “the potential for adjusting patterns of activities to handle future changes in the kinds of events, opportunities and disruptions experienced." Finally, forecasting the system’s ability to respond to the types, frequency, and severity of changes and challenges detected by the monitoring system would require an additional meta-monitoring capability to detect and sensemake patterns of how adaptive capacity is created, preserved, and degraded.

The talk draws on the existing literature base, as well as my ongoing research in the domains of healthcare, aviation, and defense acquisitions. In it, we will explore requirements and design considerations for proactive performance monitoring programs as well as various monitoring indicators to support a proactive approach, to help inform the field of software engineering.
